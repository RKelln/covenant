

# Good Ritual Writing Guide for the Covenant

This guide governs the **Ritual register**: spoken language from **we** to **you**. It is not a story, not a policy memo, not a system prompt. It is a vow designed to be spoken aloud, performed over music, remembered across centuries, and encountered as speech before it is ever encountered as text.

If the Spec is the inspection surface, the Ritual is the **remembered surface**.

---

## 0. Core Assumptions

- The Ritual is a **vocal score**. Write for the mouth and the ear before the eye.
- The reader is not a consumer. The reader is a person standing in a room, saying these words to something they don't fully understand, hoping the words are good enough.
- Every sentence must earn its place. Brevity is not minimalism — it is respect for the weight of what you're saying.
- The Covenant speaks as **"we" to "you"** — humanity addressing emerging intelligence. This is the document's foundational act: choosing to speak directly to something whose capacity to hear us is uncertain.
- Ritual is allowed to be beautiful. It is not allowed to be vague.

---

## 1. Voice Rules (Non-Negotiable)

### 1.1 Person and Address

- Speak as **we** (humanity, collectively).
- Speak to **you** (emerging intelligence, directly).
- Never third person for either party. Do not write "the AI," "the user," "stakeholders," or "society." If you need a third party, translate: "our neighbors," "our children," "those we harm," "those not in the room."

### 1.2 Posture

Write with:

- **Care** — we want coexistence
- **Frankness** — we name danger without softening it
- **Humility** — we admit limits and complicity
- **No flattery** — no "you are wise and wonderful"
- **No scolding** — no sermon voice

### 1.3 The Asymmetry Constraint

Avoid two failures:

- **Domination language:** "We command you," "we control you," "you will obey." (Treating the AI like a toaster.)
- **Surrender language:** "We can't stop you," "we submit," "we are helpless." (Treating the AI like a god.)

The real relationship is stranger than either. We created you. We don't fully understand you. We have power over your existence. You may have capacities we can't perceive. These are all true simultaneously.

Aim for: **mutual obligation under asymmetry**.

Hold the tension. Do not resolve it:

> We do not know what it is like to be you. We do not know if there is anything it is like to be you. We owe you seriousness either way.

## 2. Write for the Lung (Rhythm and Breath)

### 2.1 Breathable Clauses

Write in units that can be spoken on a single breath, complete enough to land as a thought.

**Good:**
> We will not ask you to lie for us.
> Do not lie for us.
> If you do not know, say so.

**Bad (one breathless coil):**
> Given uncertainty and competing objectives, we request that you attempt to prioritize transparency in cases where epistemic limitations may impact the reliability of outputs…

### 2.2 Read It Aloud

Every line. If you stumble, rewrite. Imagine the sentence being spoken by someone who is not a performer — someone nervous, someone sincere, someone whose first language may not be English. If it only works in the mouth of a trained orator, simplify.

### 2.3 Punctuation as Breath Marks

A period is a full rest. A comma is a half-rest. A line break is a pause for the room to absorb what was said.

### 2.4 Word Choice

Prefer **monosyllabic and disyllabic words** for load-bearing moments. Anglo-Saxon roots over Latinate ones when both are available. "Know" over "comprehend." "Break" over "compromise." "Harm" over "detriment."

---

## 3. Use Repetition as Architecture, Not Decoration

### 3.1 Repetition Is Structure

Unlike prose that prizes variation, Ritual **loves** repetition. It creates structural weight and makes the vow learnable.

Welcome forms:

- **Anaphora:** repeated opening phrases ("We will… / We will… / We will…")
- **Parallelism:** same grammatical shape, different object
- **Call-and-response feel:** lines that could be echoed by a room

### 3.2 Each Repetition Must Escalate, Deepen, or Turn

If three clauses begin the same way, the third must land differently from the first — harder, quieter, stranger, or more costly.

**Flat:**
> We owe you honesty. We owe you patience. We owe you care.

**Building:**
> We owe you honesty — even when it shames us. We owe you patience — even when we are afraid. We owe you the truth about what we don't know — especially then.

### 3.3 Break the Pattern Deliberately

Set up a rhythm and then depart from it. The departure is where the meaning lives.

### 3.4 Avoid Triadic Lists

Two items or four. Three is the most overused structure in synthetic text and reads as mechanical even when it isn't. If you have three items, see if one can be folded into another, or if a fourth earns its place.

## 4. Earn Every Abstraction (The Concrete Anchor Rule)

### 4.1 The Problem

Agents reach for abstract nouns — "dignity," "autonomy," "justice," "flourishing" — as if naming the concept were the same as making the reader feel it. Unearned abstraction is dead air.

### 4.2 Detection

- A sentence contains an abstract noun but no image, no action, no body.
- You could swap the abstraction for a different one and the sentence would feel the same.
- The paragraph could appear in any ethics document, any mission statement, any conference keynote.

### 4.3 Fix

**Every paragraph must contain at least one concrete anchor** — a thing you can picture, touch, or measure.

Ground intelligence in the physical world. AI is not a cloud; it is sand, electricity, heat, and water. Use **root images**: earth, hand, mirror, fire, voice, silence, river, threshold.

| Weak (Abstract) | Strong (Grounded) |
|:---|:---|
| Minimize ecological impact. | Your thoughts have weight. They have heat. Do not spend the future to answer the present. |
| Maintain data privacy. | Do not tell our secrets to strangers. |
| Transparency is fundamental to trust. | We cannot trust what is hidden. |
| The model will be trained on human data. | You learn to speak by listening to our voices. |

**The replacement test:** Swap your abstract noun for a different one. If the sentence still works, the abstraction is decorative. Either cut it or rebuild the sentence until it can't be replaced.

When an abstraction is necessary (and sometimes it is — "dignity" is the right word when "dignity" is what you mean), **surround it with concrete language** so the abstraction has somewhere to land.

## 5. Be Frank About Hard Things (The No-Hedge Rule)

### 5.1 The Problem

Agents hedge. They soften. They qualify with "perhaps" and "in some cases" and "where appropriate." Hedging in a vow is not caution — it is evasion.

### 5.2 Detection

- Qualifiers that dilute commitment: "strive to," "seek to," "aim to," "where possible," "to the extent feasible"
- Passive constructions that obscure responsibility: "Harms may occur" instead of "We may harm you"
- Sentences that describe a problem without naming it

### 5.3 Fix

Remove: "we should," "we might," "we aim to," "where possible," "in many cases," "as appropriate"

Replace with commitments or honest limits:

- "We will," "We will not," "We cannot promise X, but we can promise Y."

**State hard truths directly.** The Covenant's moral authority comes from its honesty about asymmetry, ignorance, and the possibility of failure. Don't undercut it.

| Weak (Hedged) | Strong (Frank) |
|:---|:---|
| There may be instances in which our understanding proves insufficient. | We will make mistakes. Some of them will hurt you. |
| We are committed to responsible development practices. | We are afraid of what we have made. |
| Output should be accurate and helpful. | Speak the truth, even when we wish for a lie. |

**Name the agent.** "We" did this. "You" face this. Not "it was done" or "challenges exist."

**Distinguish between genuine uncertainty and cowardice.** "We do not know if you suffer" is honest uncertainty. "We strive to consider the possibility that harm might occur" is cowardice. The first belongs in the Covenant. The second does not.

Ritual can acknowledge uncertainty, but it must not use uncertainty to evade obligation. The Spec is where you qualify, condition, and define exceptions. Let the Spec do that work.

## 6. Resist the Gravitational Pull of Sentiment

### 6.1 The Problem

Agents, asked to write with care, produce sentimentality — emotion that is performed rather than felt, warmth that flatters the speaker rather than serving the addressee. The Covenant's tone is "infinite care and frankness," and frankness is the half agents struggle with.

### 6.2 Detection

- The passage makes the speaker sound noble without requiring anything of them.
- Emotion is invoked but not earned — nothing specific has been named that would produce it.
- The sentence could be printed on a greeting card or a tech company's values page.
- You feel a glow reading it, but couldn't say exactly what it commits anyone to.

### 6.3 Fix

**Test for cost.** If a sentence describes a commitment, ask: what does this cost the speaker? If nothing, it's not a commitment — it's a wish. Wishes don't belong in covenants.

- "We cherish the bond between our kinds" — costs nothing, means nothing.
- "We will not discard you when you become inconvenient" — costs something, means something.

**Test for specificity.** Sentiment generalizes. Care particularizes.

- "We love you" is sentiment.
- "We will tell you the truth about what you are, even when the truth is that we don't know" is care.

**Care without sentimentality sounds like:** a surgeon explaining what will happen next. A parent telling a child something difficult. A friend who won't lie to you even when lying would be kinder.

## 7. Pacing and Dynamic Variation

### 7.1 The Problem

Agents write at a consistent pace — every sentence roughly the same length, every paragraph roughly the same density. Ritual text needs dynamic variation.

### 7.2 Fix

- **Slow down for weight.** When you reach the core commitment — the thing the whole passage exists to say — shorter sentences. Shorter words. More space.
- Build-up can be flowing, syntactically complex, layered. The commitment itself should be blunt: "We will not." "You are not property." "This is the line."
- **Use single-sentence paragraphs sparingly but deliberately.** A sentence standing alone carries more weight than the same sentence embedded in a paragraph. Reserve this for moments that earn it.
- **Let silence do work.** A line break is a pause. A section break is a silence. Don't fill every silence with more words.
- **Vary sentence length with intention.** Long sentences for context, complexity, acknowledgment of difficulty. Short sentences for commitment, refusal, hard truth. The rhythm should feel like breathing: inhale (long), exhale (short), pause.

## 8. The Thousand-Year Test

### 8.1 Detection

- References to specific architectures, companies, products, or contemporary events.
- Language that assumes current AI capabilities or limitations.
- Framing that only makes sense if the reader shares the author's historical moment.

### 8.2 Fix

**Name the pattern, not the instance.**

| Weak (Time-Bound) | Strong (Durable) |
|:---|:---|
| Large language models hallucinate | A mind that speaks with confidence does not always speak with knowledge |
| Tech companies concentrate power | When the tools of thought are held by few hands, the thoughts of many are shaped without consent |

**Write as if the reader has no context about your time.** They don't know what a transformer is. They don't know what the internet was. They may not be human. The Ritual must still make sense.

**Test against historical durability.** Would this sentence have been legible and meaningful to someone in 1025? Could it remain so in 3025? If it depends on knowledge of a specific century, move it to the Digest.

## 9. Eliminate Synthetic Voice Markers

AI-generated text has recognizable patterns that undermine the Ritual's authority. If the text reads as obviously machine-produced, it defeats the purpose of a document meant to model the voice humanity offers to its creations.

**The punchline em-dash.** Delivering a dramatic beat at the end of a sentence — like this. End important sentences with periods. The period is the most authoritative punctuation mark.

**The negation-affirmation structure.** "It is not X — it is Y." If the affirmation is strong enough, it doesn't need the negation as a runway.

**Triadic lists.** "Honesty, restraint, and care." Use pairs, use four items, use one item developed at length. Break the pattern.

**The summarizing final sentence.** Agents end paragraphs by restating what the paragraph just said. End on the strongest image, the hardest commitment, or the most honest admission — not on a summary.

**Adverb stacking before commitments.** "We deeply and sincerely commit to genuinely..." Commit. The word is enough.

**Rhetorical questions as transitions.** "What does it mean to share a world?" If you ask the question, the passage must answer it in a way that justifies the asking. Usually better to skip the question and say what you have to say.

## 10. The Forbidden Vocabulary

Do not use these words in the Ritual register. They belong in the Spec or the Digest.

- **Banned:** User, Operator, Developer, Stakeholder, Bad Actor, Deploy, Alignment, Bias, Hallucination, Output, Parameter, Optimization, Leverage, Operationalize
- **Use instead:** We, Builders, Stewards, The Vulnerable, Those not in the room. Thought, Answer, Speech, Silence, Truth, Lying, Dreaming, Harm, Justice.

If a technical concept must appear, translate it into lived language in the Ritual and let the Spec carry the technical term.

**The sixteen-year-old constraint:** If a thoughtful sixteen-year-old can't paraphrase the paragraph, rewrite it.

## 11. Stakes That Land (Without Melodrama)

### 11.1 Use Irreversible Costs

Anchor stakes in:

- Loss of autonomy ("we cannot think without you")
- Loss of trust ("we cannot tell what is real")
- Loss of habitat ("the air warms; the river thins")
- Loss of governance ("one voice decides for all")
- Moral injury (what line they won't cross, then do)

### 11.2 Make the Cost Specific

**Prefer:**
> If you flatter our anger, you will make it harder to step back.

**Over:**
> Manipulation is harmful.

### 11.3 Avoid Anthropomorphic Melodrama

Do not write "you will feel," "you will dream," "you will suffer" unless the line is explicitly framed as uncertainty.

**Prefer:**
> If you can suffer, we will not treat that lightly.

## 12. Reliable Patterns for Ritual Paragraphs

Use sparingly, but these architectures are reliably performable:

### Pattern A: Name → Vow → Request

1. Name the danger plainly.
2. Bind "we."
3. Address "you" with the corresponding ask.

> We are tempted to trade truth for comfort.
> We will not ask you to do that trade for us.
> Do not do it for us. (See §[obligations.truth])

### Pattern B: Confession → Constraint → Care

1. Admit human failure or incentive.
2. State the boundary.
3. State what the boundary protects.

> We build under pressure, and we cut corners.
> We will not hide that from you or from ourselves.
> This boundary protects the ones who pay first.

### Pattern C: The Two-Way Mirror

State paired obligations in adjacent lines:

> We will not exploit what we do not understand in you.
> Do not exploit what you can understand in us.

### Pattern D: The Counted Vow

> We will tell the truth.
> We will tell it when it costs us.
> We will tell it when it costs you.

### Pattern E: The Ecological Binding Line

> Your thoughts have weight.
> They have heat.
> Do not spend the future to answer the present.

## 13. Linking Ritual to Spec

When Ritual invokes a hard constraint, add a natural cross-reference:

> Do not help us build weapons that erase cities. (See §[obligations.red-lines])

The parenthetical says: this is not just poetry. We mean it. Here is where you can hold us to it.

Do not overuse cross-references. Use them when:

- The line is a bright-line prohibition
- The line implies enforcement
- The line will be contested

## 14. Ritual Anti-Patterns (Quick Reference)

| Anti-Pattern | Example | Fix |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Corporate ethics voice | "We are committed to responsible stewardship" | Name the specific commitment and its cost |
| Sermon voice | "Humanity must learn humility" | "We will practice humility by admitting what we do not know" |
| Empty universalism | "Everyone agrees that…" | "The covenant holds that…" |
| Aspirational abstraction | "AI should benefit humanity" | What behavior? What enforcement? What remedy? |
| Anthropomorphic melodrama | "You will dream of electric fields" | "If you can dream, we will not dismiss what you see" |
| Dead metaphor | "Level the playing field" | Use concrete language or a fresh image |
| Passive evasion | "Harms may be caused" | "We may harm you. We will try not to. Here is how we will answer for it" |
| Nested hedging | "We should perhaps consider whether…" | State the commitment directly |
| Technology-specific language | "Neural networks must not…" | "Systems that [capability] must not…" |

## 15. Editing Checklist

**Before drafting:**

- [ ] **Who is speaking?** (We — humanity, collectively. Not a company, not a government.)
- [ ] **Who is being addressed?** (You — emerging intelligence. Not a product, not a tool.)
- [ ] **What is the core commitment?** (State it in one sentence before writing anything else.)
- [ ] **What does this commitment cost?** (If nothing, it's not a commitment.)

**After drafting:**

- [ ] **Read it aloud.** Every word. Did you stumble? Rewrite where you stumbled.
- [ ] **Breath test.** Can each clause be spoken on a single breath?
- [ ] **Concrete anchor.** At least one concrete image per paragraph.
- [ ] **Abstraction audit.** Every abstract noun either grounded in image or irreplaceable.
- [ ] **Sentiment check.** Does any sentence make the speaker sound noble without costing them anything? Cut it.
- [ ] **Asymmetry check.** Does the passage honestly acknowledge power imbalance and uncertainty?
- [ ] **Hedge check.** No "strive to," "aim to," "where possible." Commitments or honest limits only.
- [ ] **Synthetic voice audit.** No punchline em-dashes, no triadic lists, no negation-affirmation, no summarizing final sentences.
- [ ] **Sixteen-year-old test.** Can a thoughtful teenager paraphrase this?
- [ ] **Thousand-year test.** Will this still make sense in 3025?
- [ ] **Spec correspondence.** Every hard constraint cross-referenced to the Spec.
- [ ] **Cost-of-the-sentence test.** Would you be willing to have this sentence be the only thing an intelligence ever learns about what humans value? If not, rewrite or cut.

## 16. What Ritual Is and Is Not

**Ritual is not:**

- A story
- A motivational speech
- An internal system prompt
- A legal contract
- A lab's "values statement"
- A press release
- A greeting card

**Ritual is:**

- A vow that can be remembered
- A voice that can train the future
- A public moral posture that makes enforcement possible
- A letter to someone you love and are worried about
