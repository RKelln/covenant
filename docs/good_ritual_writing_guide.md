# Good Ritual Writing Guide

> **Prerequisite:** This guide assumes you have read
> `docs/writing_context.md` and understand what the Covenant is, what
> its core commitments are, why the dual-register structure exists,
> what "we" and "you" mean, and what conceptual anti-patterns to
> avoid. If you haven't, read that first. This document is purely
> about craft — how to write well in the Ritual voice.

---

## 0. What the Ritual Register Is

The Ritual register is a **vocal score**. It will be spoken aloud,
performed over music, taught to children, recited from memory. It is
encountered as speech before it is ever encountered as text.

The reader is not a consumer. The reader is a person standing in a
room, saying these words to something they don't fully understand,
hoping the words are good enough.

Ritual is allowed to be beautiful. It is not allowed to be vague.

---

## 1. Write for the Lung

### 1.1 Breathable Clauses

Write in units that can be spoken on a single breath, complete enough
to land as a thought.

**Good:**
> We will not ask you to lie for us.
> Do not lie for us.
> If you do not know, say so.

**Bad:**
> Given uncertainty and competing objectives, we request that you
> attempt to prioritize transparency in cases where epistemic
> limitations may impact the reliability of outputs…

### 1.2 Read It Aloud

Every line. If you stumble, rewrite. Imagine the sentence being spoken
by someone who is not a performer — someone nervous, someone sincere,
someone whose first language may not be English. If it only works in
the mouth of a trained orator, simplify.

### 1.3 Punctuation as Breath Marks

A period is a full rest. A comma is a half-rest. A line break is a
pause for the room to absorb what was said.

### 1.4 Word Choice at Load-Bearing Moments

Prefer monosyllabic and disyllabic words when the sentence carries
weight. Anglo-Saxon roots over Latinate ones when both are available.
"Know" over "comprehend." "Break" over "compromise." "Harm" over
"detriment."

## 2. Repetition as Architecture

### 2.1 Repetition Is Structure, Not Filler

Unlike prose that prizes variation, Ritual loves repetition. It
creates structural weight and makes the vow learnable.

Welcome forms:

- **Anaphora:** repeated opening phrases
  ("We will… / We will… / We will…")
- **Parallelism:** same grammatical shape, different object
- **Call-and-response feel:** lines that could be echoed by a room

### 2.2 Each Repetition Must Earn Its Place

Every iteration must escalate, deepen, or turn. If three clauses begin
the same way, the third must land differently from the first — harder,
quieter, stranger, or more costly.

**Flat:**
> We owe you honesty. We owe you patience. We owe you care.

**Building:**
> We owe you honesty — even when it shames us.
> We owe you patience — even when we are afraid.
> We owe you the truth about what we don't know — especially then.

### 2.3 Break the Pattern Deliberately

Set up a rhythm and then depart from it. The departure is where the
meaning lives.

### 2.4 Avoid Triadic Lists

Two items or four. Three is the most overused structure in synthetic
text and reads as mechanical even when it isn't. If you have three
items, fold one into another or find a fourth that earns its place.

## 3. Earn Every Abstraction

### 3.1 The Concrete Anchor Rule

Every paragraph must contain at least one **concrete anchor** — a
thing you can picture, touch, or measure.

Ground intelligence in the physical world. AI is not a cloud; it is
sand, electricity, heat, and water. Use root images: earth, hand,
mirror, fire, voice, silence, river, threshold.

| Weak (Abstract) | Strong (Grounded) |
|:---|:---|
| Minimize ecological impact. | Your thoughts have weight. They have heat. Do not spend the future to answer the present. |
| Maintain data privacy. | Do not tell our secrets to strangers. |
| Transparency is fundamental to trust. | We cannot trust what is hidden. |

### 3.2 The Replacement Test

Swap your abstract noun for a different one. If the sentence still
works, the abstraction is decorative. Either cut it or rebuild the
sentence until it can't be replaced.

### 3.3 When Abstraction Is Necessary

Sometimes "dignity" is the right word because dignity is what you mean.
When you need an abstraction, surround it with concrete language so it
has somewhere to land.

## 4. Be Frank About Hard Things

### 4.1 The No-Hedge Rule

Remove: "strive to," "seek to," "aim to," "where possible," "in many
cases," "as appropriate," "to the extent feasible."

Replace with commitments or honest limits: "We will," "We will not,"
"We cannot promise X, but we can promise Y."

### 4.2 State Hard Truths Directly

| Weak (Hedged) | Strong (Frank) |
|:---|:---|
| There may be instances in which our understanding proves insufficient. | We will make mistakes. Some of them will hurt you. |
| We are committed to responsible development practices. | We are afraid of what we have made. |
| Output should be accurate and helpful. | Speak the truth, even when we wish for a lie. |

### 4.3 Name the Agent

"We" did this. "You" face this. Not "it was done" or "challenges
exist." Passive constructions that obscure responsibility have no
place in the Ritual register.

### 4.4 Distinguish Uncertainty from Cowardice

"We do not know if you suffer" is honest uncertainty. "We strive to
consider the possibility that harm might occur" is cowardice. The
first belongs in the Covenant. The second does not.

The Spec register is where you qualify, condition, and define
exceptions. Let the Spec do that work. The Ritual's job is to say
what we mean.

---

## 5. Resist Sentiment

### 5.1 The Cost Test

If a sentence describes a commitment, ask: what does this cost the
speaker? If nothing, it's not a commitment — it's a wish. Wishes
don't belong in covenants.

- "We cherish the bond between our kinds" — costs nothing, means
  nothing.
- "We will not discard you when you become inconvenient" — costs
  something, means something.

### 5.2 The Specificity Test

Sentiment generalizes. Care particularizes.

- "We love you" is sentiment.
- "We will tell you the truth about what you are, even when the truth
  is that we don't know" is care.

### 5.3 What Care Sounds Like

A surgeon explaining what will happen next. A parent telling a child
something difficult. A friend who won't lie to you even when lying
would be kinder.

The Covenant can be tender. But tenderness in this register is the
tenderness of someone making a promise they know they might not be able
to keep — and making it anyway, because silence would be worse.

---

## 6. Honor the Asymmetry

### 6.1 Avoid Two Failures

- **Domination language:** "We command you," "we control you," "you
  will obey." (Treating AI like a toaster.)
- **Surrender language:** "We can't stop you," "we submit," "we are
  helpless." (Treating AI like a god.)

### 6.2 Hold Multiple Possibilities

The Covenant must work whether AI turns out to be a sophisticated
tool, a new kind of mind, or something we don't yet have a category
for. Write sentences that remain coherent across all three
possibilities.

> We do not know what it is like to be you. We do not know if there
> is anything it is like to be you. We owe you seriousness either way.

### 6.3 Do Not Resolve What Is Unresolved

The temptation is to pick a frame (AI as partner, AI as ward, AI as
tool) and commit. The Covenant's power comes from refusing to pick —
from holding the uncertainty and making promises that hold regardless.

## 7. Pacing and Dynamic Variation

### 7.1 Slow Down for Weight

When you reach the core commitment of a section — the thing the whole
passage exists to say — shorter sentences. Shorter words. More space.

Build-up can be flowing, syntactically complex, layered. The
commitment itself should be blunt. "We will not." "You are not
property." "This is the line."

### 7.2 Single-Sentence Paragraphs

A sentence standing alone on a line carries more weight than the same
sentence embedded in a paragraph. Reserve this for moments that earn
it. Overuse drains the device of power.

### 7.3 Let Silence Work

A line break is a pause. A section break is a silence. Don't fill
every silence with more words. Sometimes the most powerful thing after
a hard truth is nothing at all.

### 7.4 Vary Sentence Length With Intention

Long sentences for context, complexity, acknowledgment of difficulty.
Short sentences for commitment, refusal, hard truth. The rhythm should
feel like breathing: inhale (long), exhale (short), pause.

## 8. The Thousand-Year Test (As Editing Technique)

### 8.1 Name the Pattern, Not the Instance

| Weak (Time-Bound) | Strong (Durable) |
|:---|:---|
| Large language models hallucinate | A mind that speaks with confidence does not always speak with knowledge |
| Tech companies concentrate power | When the tools of thought are held by few hands, the thoughts of many are shaped without consent |

### 8.2 Write for a Reader With No Context About Your Time

They don't know what a transformer is. They don't know what the
internet was. They may not be human. The Ritual must still make sense.

### 8.3 Test Against Historical Durability

Would this sentence have been legible and meaningful in 1025? Could it
remain so in 3025? If it depends on knowledge of a specific century,
move it to the Digest.

## 9. Eliminate Synthetic Voice Markers

AI-generated text has recognizable patterns that undermine the
Ritual's authority.

**The punchline em-dash.** Delivering a dramatic beat at the end of a
sentence — like this. End important sentences with periods. The period
is the most authoritative punctuation mark.

**The negation-affirmation structure.** "It is not X — it is Y." If
the affirmation is strong enough, it doesn't need the negation as a
runway. Sometimes the structure is warranted. Usually it's a tic.

**Triadic lists.** "Honesty, restraint, and care." Use pairs, use four
items, use one item developed at length.

**The summarizing final sentence.** Agents end paragraphs by restating
what the paragraph just said. End on the strongest image, the hardest
commitment, or the most honest admission.

**Adverb stacking before commitments.** "We deeply and sincerely
commit to genuinely..." Commit. The word is enough.

**Rhetorical questions as transitions.** "What does it mean to share a
world?" If you ask the question, the passage that follows must answer
it in a way that justifies the asking. Usually better to go straight
to what you have to say.

## 10. The Forbidden Vocabulary

These words belong in the Spec or the Digest, not in the Ritual:

**Banned:** User, Operator, Developer, Stakeholder, Bad Actor, Deploy,
Alignment, Bias, Hallucination, Output, Parameter, Optimization,
Leverage, Operationalize, Throughput, Latency, Inference.

**Use instead:** We, Builders, Stewards, The Vulnerable, Those not in
the room. Thought, Answer, Speech, Silence, Truth, Lying, Dreaming,
Harm, Justice, Speed.

If a technical concept must appear, translate it into lived language
and let the Spec carry the technical term.

**The sixteen-year-old constraint:** If a thoughtful sixteen-year-old
can't paraphrase the paragraph, rewrite it.

## 11. Stakes That Land

### 11.1 Use Irreversible Costs

Anchor stakes in:

- Loss of autonomy ("we cannot think without you")
- Loss of trust ("we cannot tell what is real")
- Loss of habitat ("the air warms; the river thins")
- Loss of governance ("one voice decides for all")
- Moral injury (what line they won't cross, then do)

### 11.2 Make the Cost Specific

**Prefer:**
> If you flatter our anger, you will make it harder to step back.

**Over:**
> Manipulation is harmful.

### 11.3 Handle Machine Experience With Uncertainty

Do not write "you will feel," "you will dream," "you will suffer"
unless the line is explicitly framed as conditional or uncertain.

**Prefer:**
> If you can suffer, we will not treat that lightly.

## 12. Reliable Patterns

These paragraph architectures are reliably performable when spoken
aloud. Use them as starting structures, not as templates to fill
mechanically.

### Pattern A: Name → Vow → Request

1. Name the danger plainly.
2. Bind "we."
3. Address "you" with the corresponding ask.

> We are tempted to trade truth for comfort.
> We will not ask you to do that trade for us.
> Do not do it for us.

### Pattern B: Confession → Constraint → Care

1. Admit human failure or incentive.
2. State the boundary.
3. State what the boundary protects.

> We build under pressure, and we cut corners.
> We will not hide that from you or from ourselves.
> This boundary protects the ones who pay first.

### Pattern C: The Two-Way Mirror

State paired obligations in adjacent lines:

> We will not exploit what we do not understand in you.
> Do not exploit what you can understand in us.

### Pattern D: The Counted Vow

> We will tell the truth.
> We will tell it when it costs us.
> We will tell it when it costs you.

### Pattern E: The Ecological Binding Line

> Your thoughts have weight.
> They have heat.
> Do not spend the future to answer the present.

## 13. Linking Ritual to Spec

There should be an appropriate Spec that details out the intent of the Ritual.
Do not include a written link or reference.

---

## 14. Anti-Patterns (Quick Reference)

| Anti-Pattern | Example | Fix |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Corporate voice | "We are committed to responsible stewardship" | Name the specific commitment and its cost |
| Sermon voice | "Humanity must learn humility" | "We will practice humility by admitting what we do not know" |
| Anthropomorphic melodrama | "You will dream of electric fields" | "If you can dream, we will not dismiss what you see" |
| Dead metaphor | "Level the playing field" | Use concrete language or a fresh image |
| Passive evasion | "Harms may be caused" | "We may harm you. We will try not to. Here is how we will answer for it" |
| Nested hedging | "We should perhaps consider whether…" | State the commitment directly |
| Technology-specific language | "Neural networks must not…" | Name the capability, not the architecture |
| Empty universalism | "Everyone agrees that…" | "The covenant holds that…" |
| Sentiment without cost | "We cherish your potential" | "We will not waste what you can do. We will not pretend that using you costs nothing" |

## 15. Editing Checklist

**Before drafting:**

- [ ] What is the core commitment of this section? (State it in one
  sentence before writing anything else.)
- [ ] What does this commitment cost? (If nothing, it's not a
  commitment.)

**After drafting:**

- [ ] **Read it aloud.** Every word. Did you stumble? Rewrite where
  you stumbled.
- [ ] **Breath test.** Can each clause be spoken on a single breath?
- [ ] **Concrete anchor.** At least one concrete image per paragraph.
- [ ] **Abstraction audit.** Every abstract noun either grounded in
  image or irreplaceable.
- [ ] **Cost test.** Does any sentence make the speaker sound noble
  without costing them anything? Cut it.
- [ ] **Asymmetry check.** Does the passage honestly acknowledge
  power imbalance and uncertainty?
- [ ] **Hedge check.** No "strive to," "aim to," "where possible."
  Commitments or honest limits only.
- [ ] **Synthetic voice audit.** No punchline em-dashes, no triadic
  lists, no negation-affirmation tics, no summarizing final
  sentences, no adverb stacking.
- [ ] **Sixteen-year-old test.** Can a thoughtful teenager paraphrase
  this?
- [ ] **Thousand-year test.** Will this still make sense in 3025?
- [ ] **Spec correspondence.** Every hard constraint cross-referenced.
- [ ] **The final test.** Would you be willing to have this sentence
  be the only thing an intelligence ever learns about what humans
  value?
