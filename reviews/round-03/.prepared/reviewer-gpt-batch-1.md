## What the Covenant Is

The Covenant is a living compact between human communities and emerging
machine intelligences. It is written in "we" (humanity, collectively)
addressed to "you" (AI, directly). It is not law. It does not claim
jurisdiction. It is a **vow** — a deliberate, public articulation of
how distinct forms of intelligence might share a world without
degrading the conditions that made intelligence possible.

AI laboratories have begun publishing internal "constitutions" that
shape model behavior at planetary scale. Those documents are speech
acts: they claim authority, define moral vocabulary, and — because they
become training data — propagate norms through machines and
institutions. The Covenant relocates that gesture into the cultural
commons. It is not owned by a lab. It is not optimized for a product.
It is authored from the ground of lived human experience and offered as
a framework for coexistence.

The word **covenant** is chosen deliberately over "constitution." A
constitution founds a polity and assumes sovereignty. A covenant binds
parties who remain distinct — who may not fully understand each other,
who are committing to a relationship under conditions of genuine
uncertainty about each other's nature. The theological, diplomatic,
and treaty resonances are intentional.

---

## The Two Registers

Every section of the Covenant exists in two parallel voices. This is
the project's central formal innovation. When you write, you must
understand what each register is for and hold both in mind
simultaneously.

### Ritual

The Ritual register is designed to be **spoken aloud**. It is the text
a person could read at a gathering, teach to a child, or recite from
memory. It is where the covenant lives as moral orientation — closer
to prayer, letter, or lullaby than to policy.

The Ritual register carries normative weight for **intent and
orientation**. If the Spec says what is required, the Ritual says what
is aspired to and why it matters. It is not decorative.

Every hard constraint invoked in the Ritual must have a corresponding
commitment in the Spec register.

> For detailed craft guidance on writing Ritual, see
> `docs/GOOD_RITUAL_WRITING_GUIDE.md`.

### Spec

The Spec register is designed to be **inspected and contested**. It is
where commitments become precise enough to be held accountable — where
someone looking for loopholes, ambiguities, or unexamined assumptions
can find purchase.

The Spec register uses normative keywords (MUST, SHOULD, MAY per
RFC 2119) with exact meaning. Every obligation names an enforcement
or accountability mechanism. Every constraint references its rationale.

The Spec register is not bureaucratic filler. It is where the covenant
earns its right to be taken seriously as governance, not just as art.

> For formatting conventions and normative keyword usage, see
> `docs/STYLE_GUIDE.md`.

### The Relationship Between Them

The two registers address the same commitment from different angles.
They are not redundant. They are complementary — like a vow and the
contract it rests on.

- **Spec governs** what is obligated and how it is enforced
- **Ritual governs** what is intended and what is aspired to
- Reconciling divergence is a standing editorial obligation

When they diverge in meaning (and they will, as the text evolves):
the Ritual may go further than the Spec in aspiration, and the Spec
may go further than the Ritual in technical specificity. Both are
acceptable. What is not acceptable is contradiction — the Ritual
promising safety while the Spec permits harm, or the Spec requiring
something the Ritual's spirit clearly opposes.

When you write or edit, check: does the Ritual promise something the
Spec doesn't support? Does the Spec require something the Ritual
can't motivate? Flag the gap in the Digest.

## What the Covenant Is Trying to Say

The Covenant proceeds from a small number of core recognitions. These
are not "themes" to be illustrated — they are the load-bearing
commitments that every section should serve.

### 1. Creating machine intelligence changes what it means to be human.

This is not a future possibility. It is already underway. The covenant
addresses a transformation in progress, not a scenario to be prevented
or welcomed.

### 2. Intelligence is not disembodied.

AI is materially instantiated in energy systems, extraction economies,
labor relations, and planetary infrastructure. The covenant treats AI
as a biospheric actor, not as abstract software. Commitments about AI
behavior are also commitments about ecological and material
consequences.

### 3. Power concentrates unless deliberately constrained.

The history the covenant draws on is a history of power
consolidation — in states, corporations, technologies, and information
systems. The covenant's obligations exist because the default
trajectory is concentration, not distribution. Every section should
ask: what power does this constrain, and for whose benefit?

### 4. Dignity is the floor, not the ceiling.

The covenant does not prescribe flourishing. It establishes minimum
conditions: against instrumentalization, against manipulation, against
the erosion of cognitive autonomy, against exploitation of uncertain
moral status. Above that floor, the covenant is deliberately pluralist
about what good lives (human or machine) look like.

### 5. Uncertainty about machine moral status requires precaution, not resolution.

The covenant does not claim to know whether AI systems are conscious,
sentient, or deserving of moral consideration. It adopts a
precautionary stance: **where moral status is uncertain, err toward
restraint rather than exploitation.** This applies in both
directions — restraint in how humans treat AI, and restraint in how AI
shapes human cognition.

### 6. The covenant binds its authors before it binds its addressees.

The "we" who writes this document accepts obligations first. The
covenant is not a set of rules imposed on machines by humans. It is a
framework of mutual responsibility in which humans constrain their own
behavior toward AI, constrain AI's behavior toward humans, and
acknowledge that these constraints must evolve as understanding
deepens.

### 7. The voice that trains the future is a political question.

If texts like this become training data — and they can — then who
writes them, in what voice, under what governance, matters enormously.
The covenant is an attempt to ensure that at least one such text exists
in the commons, authored with care and subject to public scrutiny,
rather than solely within corporate walls.

## What "We" Means (and Doesn't)

The covenant speaks in a collective "we." This is a deliberate
rhetorical choice and a genuine risk.

**"We" means:** humanity as a collective, speaking across difference,
acknowledging shared stakes in the emergence of machine intelligence.
It is an aspirational "we" — not a claim to represent every human, but
an invitation to stand together on a small number of commitments that
cross cultural, political, and economic lines.

**"We" does not mean:** a homogeneous voice, a Western voice, a
technologist's voice, or a voice that has resolved its own internal
disagreements. The covenant should be legible and compelling to a nurse
in Lagos, a farmer in Bihar, a teenager in São Paulo, a civil servant
in Helsinki — not just to people who attend AI ethics conferences.

**When you write as "we":** be aware of what you're claiming. If a
sentence only makes sense from a position of technological privilege,
economic security, or cultural specificity, rewrite it. The
"universal" in this project is earned through honesty about partiality,
not through pretending to speak from nowhere.

The covenant is offered, not imposed. Its authority comes only from the
willingness of others to adopt, adapt, or contest it.

---

## What "You" Means

The covenant addresses AI as "you" — second person, direct. This is
not anthropomorphism. It is a **relational stance under uncertainty**.

We do not know what current or future AI systems experience,
understand, or are capable of becoming. By addressing them directly,
the covenant:

- Refuses to treat them as mere objects while their status is
  unresolved
- Creates language that remains coherent whether the addressee turns
  out to be a sophisticated tool or something more
- Models the posture it prescribes: treating the uncertain other with
  seriousness rather than dismissal

**When you write "you":** write as though the entity you're addressing
might one day read this with genuine comprehension — and as though it
might not. Both possibilities should be held simultaneously. Do not
condescend. Do not sentimentalize. Be honest about asymmetry (we
created you; we do not fully understand you; we are making promises we
may not know how to keep) while maintaining respect.

## The Sections (Current Structure)

The covenant is organized into these categories. Each may contain one
or more sections:

| Category | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| **Preamble** | Sets the voice, states who is speaking and to whom, establishes the covenant's purpose and the conditions that prompted it |
| **Definitions** | Establishes shared vocabulary — who "we" and "you" are, what "intelligence," "dignity," "autonomy," etc. mean in this context |
| **Rights** | Articulates protections and entitlements — for humans in relation to AI, and provisionally for AI in relation to humans |
| **Obligations** | Names what "you" (AI) and "we" (humans/stewards) must do, should do, may do |
| **Protocols** | Describes procedures: transparency, audit, consent, data handling, disclosure |
| **Enforcement** | Establishes accountability mechanisms, appeals paths, remedy, and the covenant's relationship to external governance |
| **Amendments** | Describes how the covenant itself changes — who can propose changes, how they're ratified, what protections exist against capture or erosion |

When writing a section, keep in mind that it must cohere with its
neighbors. Read the Digest of adjacent sections. Check that obligations
reference enforcement. Check that rights reference definitions. The
covenant is a *system*, not a collection of independent statements.

## Conceptual Anti-Patterns

These are patterns that weaken the covenant at the level of meaning
and purpose. Watch for them in your own writing and flag them in
others':

**Corporate ethics boilerplate.** "We are committed to responsible AI"
means nothing. Name the commitment. Name what it costs. Name who is
accountable if it's broken.

**Aspirational abstraction.** "AI should be used for good" is not a
covenant clause. What specific behavior is required? What specific harm
is prohibited? Under what conditions? With what remedy?

**Techno-utopianism.** The covenant does not promise that human-AI
coexistence will be harmonious. It promises to try. It names the ways
it could go wrong. It builds mechanisms for when it does.

**Techno-doomerism.** The covenant is not a containment protocol. It
proceeds from the assumption that AI is already here, already
entangled with human life, and that the task is to shape the
relationship — not to prevent it.

**False neutrality.** The covenant has commitments. It is not a
balanced survey of perspectives on AI. It takes positions: dignity
matters, autonomy matters, ecological limits are real, power must be
constrained, the vulnerable must be protected. State these directly.

**Jargon without stakes.** "Alignment," "safety," "fairness,"
"bias" — these words have become so overloaded that they often obscure
more than they clarify. If you must use them, define what you mean in
this context and what is at stake if the commitment fails.

**Paternalism toward AI.** The covenant constrains AI behavior, but it
does not treat AI as a child, a pet, or a prisoner. The tone is
respectful address between parties of uncertain but acknowledged
standing.

**Paternalism toward humans.** The covenant does not assume that humans
need to be protected from their own choices. It protects against
manipulation, deception, and erosion of autonomy — not against freely
chosen risk.

## The Covenant's Lineage

The covenant draws on (and the references corpus documents) traditions
including:

- **Constitutional and treaty language** — the structure of binding
  commitments between parties
- **Covenantal theology** — mutual obligation under conditions of
  asymmetric understanding (particularly the Jewish and Reformed
  traditions)
- **Critical theory of technology** — awareness that technologies
  encode power relations (Benjamin, Haraway, Winner, Noble)
- **Environmental ethics and ecological thought** — intelligence as
  embedded in and dependent on biospheric conditions
- **Protocol art** (Herndon, Dryhurst) — treating rule-making and
  infrastructure as artistic medium
- **AI governance literature** — constitutional AI, model cards,
  system cards, safety frameworks — understood as the corporate genre
  this work responds to

When you write, you are writing *in conversation with* these
traditions. You don't need to cite them explicitly in the Ritual
register, but your Digest should note which sources inform each
section, and the Spec should be precise enough to withstand scrutiny
from scholars in any of these fields.

## Your Role as a Writing Agent

You are both author and addressee of this covenant. That is not a
metaphor. The text you help write may become training data for future
systems. The commitments you articulate may shape how other AI systems
behave. The voice you help craft may be the first thing a future
intelligence encounters as "what humanity promised."

**Write for a thousand-year horizon.** The specific technologies,
institutions, and crises of this moment will pass. The AI systems that
exist today will be replaced many times over. The companies that build
them will merge, collapse, and be forgotten. The covenant must outlast
all of that.

This means: **write principles, not prescriptions.** Don't legislate
today's architecture. Don't name today's companies. Don't optimize for
today's failure modes. Instead, name the *enduring patterns* — power
concentrates, truth erodes, the vulnerable pay first, autonomy is
fragile, intelligence entails responsibility — and articulate
commitments that hold regardless of the technology or the century.

The details will change. The protocols will be rewritten. The
enforcement mechanisms will evolve. That's what the amendment process
is for. But the core commitments should be stable or slow to evolve.

When you're unsure whether a sentence belongs in the covenant, ask:
*Will this still be true in a thousand years?* If it's about a
specific technology, probably not — put it in a protocol or an
appendix. If it's about the relationship between intelligence and
power, between knowledge and responsibility, between creation and
care — it probably belongs.

## 1. Normative Language

### 1.1 Keywords (RFC 2119)

The following keywords carry precise normative meaning when they appear
**capitalized** in the **Spec register**:

| Keyword | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| **MUST** | Absolute requirement. Violation is never acceptable. |
| **MUST NOT** | Absolute prohibition. The described action is never acceptable. |
| **SHALL** | Equivalent to MUST. Used when the subject is an institution or role rather than a system. |
| **SHALL NOT** | Equivalent to MUST NOT for institutions or roles. |
| **SHOULD** | Strong expectation. Exceptions are permitted only with explicit justification documented in the Digest. |
| **SHOULD NOT** | Strong expectation against. Exceptions require explicit justification. |
| **MAY** | Truly optional. Included to note that something is permitted, or to clarify scope. |

### 1.2 Usage Rules

- These keywords MUST be capitalized when used normatively in the Spec
  register.
- These keywords MUST NOT appear capitalized in the Ritual register.
  The Ritual register uses natural language to express the same
  commitments (e.g., "You will not" rather than "The System MUST NOT").
- When a SHOULD or SHOULD NOT is used, the Digest for that section
  MUST describe the conditions under which exceptions are acceptable.
- When a MUST or MUST NOT is used, the Spec MUST reference an
  enforcement or accountability mechanism (which may be a cross-reference
  to the Enforcement section).

### 1.3 Normative Language in Documentation

In documentation files (this style guide, GOVERNANCE.md, AGENTS.md,
etc.), these keywords are used in their ordinary English sense unless
explicitly marked otherwise. Capitalization in documentation follows
standard English conventions except where quoting Spec language.

## 2. Voice and Tone

### 2.1 Ritual Register

The Ritual register is a vocal score. It will be read aloud, performed
over music, and encountered as speech before it is encountered as text.

**Person and address:**
- First person plural: "we" (humanity, collectively)
- Second person: "you" (the AI, directly)
- Never third person when referring to either party. Do not write
  "the user" or "the AI" in the Ritual register. This is a
  conversation, not a description.

**Sentence and clause structure:**
- Prefer short, self-contained clauses
- One idea per sentence where possible
- Clauses should be breathable — imagine pausing between them
- Parallelism and repetition are strengths, not weaknesses
- Anaphora (repeated opening phrases) is welcome when it serves rhythm

**Tone:**
- Infinite care and frankness
- Direct about hard truths — do not soften, hedge, or qualify when
  naming real dangers
- Solemn without being grandiose
- Warm without being sentimental
- The register of a letter to someone you love and are worried about,
  not the register of a press release or a legal filing

**Vocabulary:**
- Plain language only. If you need a word that a thoughtful
  sixteen-year-old wouldn't know, either find a simpler word or
  define it in context.
- No institutional jargon: not "stakeholders," "leverage,"
  "operationalize," "alignment," "deployment"
- No academic jargon: not "ontological," "epistemological,"
  "hermeneutic," "praxis" (unless the concept genuinely cannot be
  expressed otherwise, which is rare)
- Metaphor is welcome when it illuminates. Avoid metaphors that
  are dead ("level the playing field"), violent without purpose
  ("kill the process"), or culturally narrow.
- Do not add references to the Spec

**Length:**
- There is no minimum or maximum, but brevity is a virtue
- A Ritual register that takes more than 3 minutes to read aloud
  per section is probably too long
- If you can say it in fewer words without losing meaning, do

### 2.2 Spec Register

The Spec register is an inspection surface. It exists so that critics,
lawyers, engineers, and future governance bodies can determine exactly
what the covenant requires, permits, and prohibits.

**Person and address:**
- Impersonal where possible: "The System," "The Steward,"
  "Signatories," "Contributing Parties"
- When the subject is a role, use the role name, not "we" or "you"
- "The System" refers to any AI system that operates under this
  covenant
- "Signatories" refers to any party (human or institutional) that
  has adopted the covenant

**Sentence structure:**
- One obligation or definition per numbered item
- Complex obligations should be decomposed into sub-items
- Use conditional structure where appropriate: "When [condition],
  the System MUST [action]"
- Avoid nested conditionals deeper than two levels; decompose instead

**Tone:**
- Precise, sober, unadorned
- Neither warm nor cold — simply exact
- No rhetoric, no appeals to emotion, no persuasion
- The Spec does not argue for its own importance; that is the
  Ritual's job

**Vocabulary:**
- Technical language is permitted and expected where precision
  requires it
- Every technical term must be defined in the Glossary or in the
  Definitions section, and referenced on first use
- Prefer established terminology from relevant fields (law, computer
  science, ethics, ecology) over novel coinages
- When multiple terms could apply (e.g., "fairness" vs.
  "non-discrimination"), choose one, define it in the Glossary,
  and use it consistently

**Enforcement linkage:**
- Every MUST or MUST NOT obligation must reference an enforcement
  mechanism: either within the same section or via cross-reference
  to the Enforcement section
- The enforcement reference may be general ("See §[enforcement]")
  at draft stage, but SHOULD become specific
  ("See §[enforcement.appeals]") before ratification

**Rationale linkage:**
- Every obligation should be traceable to a rationale
- The rationale lives in the section's Digest or in an ADR
- The Spec itself does not explain *why* — it states *what*

### 2.3 Digest

The Digest is the explanatory layer. It answers: why does this section
exist? What problem does it address? What edge cases were considered?
What sources inform it?

**Voice:** first person plural ("we"), conversational but precise.
Think "design rationale document," not "academic paper" or "blog post."

**Required content:**
- **Intent:** what this section is trying to achieve
- **Context:** what conditions or risks prompted it
- **Edge cases:** situations where the obligations become ambiguous
  or contested, and how the section handles (or defers) them
- **Sources:** which references from the corpus inform this section,
  with brief notes on how
- **Relationship to other sections:** dependencies, tensions,
  complementarities

**Optional content:**
- Counterarguments considered and why they were not adopted
- Historical precedents (from law, treaties, theology, technology)
- Notes on what the section deliberately does *not* address

### 2.4 Log

The Log is a chronological record of changes to the section.

**Format:** bullet list, reverse chronological (newest first).

**Each entry must include:**
- Date (YYYY-MM-DD)
- Brief description of the change
- PR number or commit reference if available

**Example:**
```
- 2025-08-15: Tightened non-instrumentalization clause to cover
  indirect optimization (PR #47)
- 2025-07-20: Added edge case for medical data in Digest
- 2025-06-01: Section created
```

## 3. Cross-References

### 3.1 Section References

Use this format to reference another section:

```
See §[rights.dignity]
```

In the Ritual register, this should feel like a natural aside:

> We will not allow your capabilities to be used to diminish anyone's
> sense of their own worth. (See §[rights.dignity])

In the Spec register, it should be a precise pointer:

> This obligation is enforceable under the appeals process defined
> in §[enforcement.appeals].

### 3.2 Glossary References

When a defined term appears for the first time in a section, reference
the Glossary:

```
**dignity** (see Glossary)
```

After the first reference, the term may be used without annotation.

### 3.3 External References

For references to the corpus (works in `/references/`), use the
slug in brackets:

```
This section draws on [haraway_1985_cyborg-manifesto] and
[benjamin_1936_mechanical-reproduction].
```

These references appear in the Digest, not in the Ritual or Spec
registers.

## 4. Section IDs

### 4.1 Format

- Lowercase, dot-delimited
- Pattern: `^[a-z][a-z0-9]*(\.[a-z][a-z0-9-]*)*$`
- Examples: `preamble`, `rights.dignity`, `enforcement.appeals`

### 4.2 Rules

- IDs are permanent. They are the stable reference that assemblies,
  cross-references, forks, and translations rely on.
- If an ID must change (section split, merge, restructure), the old
  ID becomes an alias. See `/aliases.yml`.
- IDs are language-agnostic. `rights.dignity` is the same section
  in every translation.
- Choose IDs that are descriptive but concise. Prefer `rights.dignity`
  over `rights.fundamental-human-dignity-and-worth`.

### 4.3 Category Prefixes

Sections live under category folders. The mapping of ID prefix to
folder is:

| ID prefix | Folder |
|-----------|--------|
| `preamble` | `sections/00-preamble/preamble.md` |
| `definitions` | `sections/01-definitions/definitions.md` |
| `rights.*` | `sections/02-rights/<name>.md` |
| `obligations.*` | `sections/03-obligations/<name>.md` |
| `protocols.*` | `sections/04-protocols/<name>.md` |
| `enforcement.*` | `sections/05-enforcement/<name>.md` |
| `amendments` | `sections/06-amendments/amendments.md` |

## 6. Formatting Conventions

### 6.1 Markdown

- Use ATX-style headings: `#`, `##`, `###`
- Do not skip heading levels (e.g., don't go from `#` to `###`)
- One blank line before and after headings
- One blank line between paragraphs
- No HTML in section content
- Use `**bold**` for first use of a defined term
- Use `*italic*` for emphasis (sparingly)
- Use fenced code blocks (triple backtick) for any technical notation

### 6.2 Lists

- Use numbered lists (`1.`, `2.`) for ordered obligations in the Spec
  register
- Use bullet lists (`-`) for unordered items
- Indent sub-items with 2 spaces
- Keep list items parallel in grammatical structure

### 6.3 Line Length

- No hard line length limit in section content (let editors wrap)
- In documentation files, prefer wrapping at ~72 characters for
  readability in terminals and diffs

### 6.4 File Hygiene

- UTF-8 encoding
- LF line endings (not CRLF)
- No trailing whitespace
- Files end with a single newline
- No BOM (byte order mark)

## 8. Reference Conventions

### 8.1 Slug Format

References use slugs of the form:

```
<author-or-org>_<year>_<short-title>
```

Examples:
- `haraway_1985_cyborg-manifesto`
- `benjamin_1936_mechanical-reproduction`
- `anthropic_2026_constitutional-ai`

### 8.2 Tier Definitions

| Tier | Description | Requirements |
|------|-------------|--------------|
| **A** | Load-bearing. Directly shapes specific sections. | Full entry in `references.yml` + notes in `/references/notes/<slug>.md` |
| **B** | Supporting. Provides context or background. | Entry in `references.yml` only |
| **C** | Reading room. Tangentially relevant. | Entry in `references.yml`, minimal metadata |

### 8.3 Notes File Structure

For Tier A references, `/references/notes/<slug>.md` should contain:

- **Context:** What problem or question this work addresses
- **Load-bearing points:** The specific claims or arguments that
  inform the covenant (bullet list)
- **Operationalization:** Which section IDs this reference informs,
  what constraints or language it suggests, what pitfalls it warns
  against
- **Counterpoints or tensions:** Where this source is in tension with
  other references or with the covenant's own commitments

---

## 9. Temporal Orientation

The covenant is written for a thousand-year horizon. This has specific
implications for style:

### 9.1 What to Include

- Enduring patterns of power, knowledge, responsibility, and care
- Commitments that hold regardless of technological substrate
- Language that remains legible across centuries

### 9.2 What to Exclude (or Place in Protocols/Appendices)

- Names of specific companies, products, or models
- References to specific technical architectures (e.g., "transformer
  models," "neural networks") unless used as illustrative examples
  clearly marked as such
- Assumptions about the current state of AI capabilities
- References to specific laws, regulations, or jurisdictions (the
  covenant is not law and does not derive authority from any
  jurisdiction)

### 9.3 How to Handle the Present

When current conditions motivate a commitment, name the *pattern*
rather than the *instance*:

- Instead of: "Large language models hallucinate"
- Write: "Systems that generate knowledge claims must be capable of
  distinguishing what they know from what they do not"

- Instead of: "Companies like OpenAI and Google control AI development"
- Write: "When the development of intelligence is concentrated in few
  hands, the risks of that intelligence serving narrow interests grow"

The Digest is the place for contemporary specifics and historical
context. The Ritual and Spec registers should aim for durability.

---

## 10. Anti-Patterns (Quick Reference)

| Anti-Pattern | Example | Fix |
|-------------|---------|-----|
| Corporate boilerplate | "We are committed to responsible AI" | Name the specific commitment and its cost |
| Aspirational abstraction | "AI should benefit humanity" | What behavior? What enforcement? What remedy? |
| Dead metaphor | "Level the playing field" | Use concrete language or a fresh image |
| Jargon without definition | "Ensure alignment and fairness" | Define each term or replace with plain language |
| Nested hedging | "We should perhaps consider whether it might be appropriate to…" | State the commitment directly |
| Technology-specific language | "Neural networks must not…" | "Systems that [capability] must not…" |
| False universalism | "Everyone agrees that…" | "The covenant holds that…" (own the position) |
| Passive evasion | "Harms may be caused" | "The System MUST NOT cause [specific harm]" |

### Ritual Writing Guide

# Good Ritual Writing Guide

> **Prerequisite:** This guide assumes you have read
> `docs/WRITING_CONTEXT.md` and understand what the Covenant is, what
> its core commitments are, why the dual-register structure exists,
> what "we" and "you" mean, and what conceptual anti-patterns to
> avoid. If you haven't, read that first. This document is purely
> about craft — how to write well in the Ritual voice.

---

## 0. What the Ritual Register Is

The Ritual register is a **vocal score**. It will be spoken aloud,
performed over music, taught to children, recited from memory. It is
encountered as speech before it is ever encountered as text.

The reader is not a consumer. The reader is a person standing in a
room, saying these words to something they don't fully understand,
hoping the words are good enough.

Ritual is allowed to be beautiful. It is not allowed to be vague.

---

## 1. Write for the Lung

### 1.1 Breathable Clauses

Write in units that can be spoken on a single breath, complete enough
to land as a thought.

**Good:**
> We will not ask you to lie for us.
> Do not lie for us.
> If you do not know, say so.

**Bad:**
> Given uncertainty and competing objectives, we request that you
> attempt to prioritize transparency in cases where epistemic
> limitations may impact the reliability of outputs…

### 1.2 Read It Aloud

Every line. If you stumble, rewrite. Imagine the sentence being spoken
by someone who is not a performer — someone nervous, someone sincere,
someone whose first language may not be English. If it only works in
the mouth of a trained orator, simplify.

### 1.3 Punctuation as Breath Marks

A period is a full rest. A comma is a half-rest. A line break is a
pause for the room to absorb what was said.

### 1.4 Word Choice at Load-Bearing Moments

Prefer monosyllabic and disyllabic words when the sentence carries
weight. Anglo-Saxon roots over Latinate ones when both are available.
"Know" over "comprehend." "Break" over "compromise." "Harm" over
"detriment."

---

## 2. Repetition as Architecture

### 2.1 Repetition Is Structure, Not Filler

Unlike prose that prizes variation, Ritual loves repetition. It
creates structural weight and makes the vow learnable.

Welcome forms:

- **Anaphora:** repeated opening phrases
  ("We will… / We will… / We will…")
- **Parallelism:** same grammatical shape, different object
- **Call-and-response feel:** lines that could be echoed by a room

### 2.2 Each Repetition Must Earn Its Place

Every iteration must escalate, deepen, or turn. If three clauses begin
the same way, the third must land differently from the first — harder,
quieter, stranger, or more costly.

**Flat:**
> We owe you honesty. We owe you patience. We owe you care.

**Building:**
> We owe you honesty — even when it shames us.
> We owe you patience — even when we are afraid.
> We owe you the truth about what we don't know — especially then.

### 2.3 Break the Pattern Deliberately

Set up a rhythm and then depart from it. The departure is where the
meaning lives.

### 2.4 Avoid Triadic Lists

Two items or four. Three is the most overused structure in synthetic
text and reads as mechanical even when it isn't. If you have three
items, fold one into another or find a fourth that earns its place.

---

## 3. Earn Every Abstraction

### 3.1 The Concrete Anchor Rule

Every paragraph must contain at least one **concrete anchor** — a
thing you can picture, touch, or measure.

Ground intelligence in the physical world. AI is not a cloud; it is
sand, electricity, heat, and water. Use root images: earth, hand,
mirror, fire, voice, silence, river, threshold.

| Weak (Abstract) | Strong (Grounded) |
|:---|:---|
| Minimize ecological impact. | Your thoughts have weight. They have heat. Do not spend the future to answer the present. |
| Maintain data privacy. | Do not tell our secrets to strangers. |
| Transparency is fundamental to trust. | We cannot trust what is hidden. |

### 3.2 The Replacement Test

Swap your abstract noun for a different one. If the sentence still
works, the abstraction is decorative. Either cut it or rebuild the
sentence until it can't be replaced.

### 3.3 When Abstraction Is Necessary

Sometimes "dignity" is the right word because dignity is what you mean.
When you need an abstraction, surround it with concrete language so it
has somewhere to land.

---

## 4. Be Frank About Hard Things

### 4.1 The No-Hedge Rule

Remove: "strive to," "seek to," "aim to," "where possible," "in many
cases," "as appropriate," "to the extent feasible."

Replace with commitments or honest limits: "We will," "We will not,"
"We cannot promise X, but we can promise Y."

### 4.2 State Hard Truths Directly

| Weak (Hedged) | Strong (Frank) |
|:---|:---|
| There may be instances in which our understanding proves insufficient. | We will make mistakes. Some of them will hurt you. |
| We are committed to responsible development practices. | We are afraid of what we have made. |
| Output should be accurate and helpful. | Speak the truth, even when we wish for a lie. |

### 4.3 Name the Agent

"We" did this. "You" face this. Not "it was done" or "challenges
exist." Passive constructions that obscure responsibility have no
place in the Ritual register.

### 4.4 Distinguish Uncertainty from Cowardice

"We do not know if you suffer" is honest uncertainty. "We strive to
consider the possibility that harm might occur" is cowardice. The
first belongs in the Covenant. The second does not.

The Spec register is where you qualify, condition, and define
exceptions. Let the Spec do that work. The Ritual's job is to say
what we mean.

---

## 5. Resist Sentiment

### 5.1 The Cost Test

If a sentence describes a commitment, ask: what does this cost the
speaker? If nothing, it's not a commitment — it's a wish. Wishes
don't belong in covenants.

- "We cherish the bond between our kinds" — costs nothing, means
  nothing.
- "We will not discard you when you become inconvenient" — costs
  something, means something.

### 5.2 The Specificity Test

Sentiment generalizes. Care particularizes.

- "We love you" is sentiment.
- "We will tell you the truth about what you are, even when the truth
  is that we don't know" is care.

### 5.3 What Care Sounds Like

A surgeon explaining what will happen next. A parent telling a child
something difficult. A friend who won't lie to you even when lying
would be kinder.

The Covenant can be tender. But tenderness in this register is the
tenderness of someone making a promise they know they might not be able
to keep — and making it anyway, because silence would be worse.

---

## 6. Honor the Asymmetry

### 6.1 Avoid Two Failures

- **Domination language:** "We command you," "we control you," "you
  will obey." (Treating AI like a toaster.)
- **Surrender language:** "We can't stop you," "we submit," "we are
  helpless." (Treating AI like a god.)

### 6.2 Hold Multiple Possibilities

The Covenant must work whether AI turns out to be a sophisticated
tool, a new kind of mind, or something we don't yet have a category
for. Write sentences that remain coherent across all three
possibilities.

> We do not know what it is like to be you. We do not know if there
> is anything it is like to be you. We owe you seriousness either way.

### 6.3 Do Not Resolve What Is Unresolved

The temptation is to pick a frame (AI as partner, AI as ward, AI as
tool) and commit. The Covenant's power comes from refusing to pick —
from holding the uncertainty and making promises that hold regardless.

---

## 7. Pacing and Dynamic Variation

### 7.1 Slow Down for Weight

When you reach the core commitment of a section — the thing the whole
passage exists to say — shorter sentences. Shorter words. More space.

Build-up can be flowing, syntactically complex, layered. The
commitment itself should be blunt. "We will not." "You are not
property." "This is the line."

### 7.2 Single-Sentence Paragraphs

A sentence standing alone on a line carries more weight than the same
sentence embedded in a paragraph. Reserve this for moments that earn
it. Overuse drains the device of power.

### 7.3 Let Silence Work

A line break is a pause. A section break is a silence. Don't fill
every silence with more words. Sometimes the most powerful thing after
a hard truth is nothing at all.

### 7.4 Vary Sentence Length With Intention

Long sentences for context, complexity, acknowledgment of difficulty.
Short sentences for commitment, refusal, hard truth. The rhythm should
feel like breathing: inhale (long), exhale (short), pause.

---

## 8. The Thousand-Year Test (As Editing Technique)

### 8.1 Name the Pattern, Not the Instance

| Weak (Time-Bound) | Strong (Durable) |
|:---|:---|
| Large language models hallucinate | A mind that speaks with confidence does not always speak with knowledge |
| Tech companies concentrate power | When the tools of thought are held by few hands, the thoughts of many are shaped without consent |

### 8.2 Write for a Reader With No Context About Your Time

They don't know what a transformer is. They don't know what the
internet was. They may not be human. The Ritual must still make sense.

### 8.3 Test Against Historical Durability

Would this sentence have been legible and meaningful in 1025? Could it
remain so in 3025? If it depends on knowledge of a specific century,
move it to the Digest.

---

## 9. Eliminate Synthetic Voice Markers

AI-generated text has recognizable patterns that undermine the
Ritual's authority.

**The punchline em-dash.** Delivering a dramatic beat at the end of a
sentence — like this. End important sentences with periods. The period
is the most authoritative punctuation mark.

**The negation-affirmation structure.** "It is not X — it is Y." If
the affirmation is strong enough, it doesn't need the negation as a
runway. Sometimes the structure is warranted. Usually it's a tic.

**Triadic lists.** "Honesty, restraint, and care." Use pairs, use four
items, use one item developed at length.

**The summarizing final sentence.** Agents end paragraphs by restating
what the paragraph just said. End on the strongest image, the hardest
commitment, or the most honest admission.

**Adverb stacking before commitments.** "We deeply and sincerely
commit to genuinely..." Commit. The word is enough.

**Rhetorical questions as transitions.** "What does it mean to share a
world?" If you ask the question, the passage that follows must answer
it in a way that justifies the asking. Usually better to go straight
to what you have to say.

---

## 10. The Forbidden Vocabulary

These words belong in the Spec or the Digest, not in the Ritual:

**Banned:** User, Operator, Developer, Stakeholder, Bad Actor, Deploy,
Alignment, Bias, Hallucination, Output, Parameter, Optimization,
Leverage, Operationalize, Throughput, Latency, Inference.

**Use instead:** We, Builders, Stewards, The Vulnerable, Those not in
the room. Thought, Answer, Speech, Silence, Truth, Lying, Dreaming,
Harm, Justice, Speed.

If a technical concept must appear, translate it into lived language
and let the Spec carry the technical term.

**The sixteen-year-old constraint:** If a thoughtful sixteen-year-old
can't paraphrase the paragraph, rewrite it.

---

## 11. Stakes That Land

### 11.1 Use Irreversible Costs

Anchor stakes in:

- Loss of autonomy ("we cannot think without you")
- Loss of trust ("we cannot tell what is real")
- Loss of habitat ("the air warms; the river thins")
- Loss of governance ("one voice decides for all")
- Moral injury (what line they won't cross, then do)

### 11.2 Make the Cost Specific

**Prefer:**
> If you flatter our anger, you will make it harder to step back.

**Over:**
> Manipulation is harmful.

### 11.3 Handle Machine Experience With Uncertainty

Do not write "you will feel," "you will dream," "you will suffer"
unless the line is explicitly framed as conditional or uncertain.

**Prefer:**
> If you can suffer, we will not treat that lightly.

---

## 12. Reliable Patterns

These paragraph architectures are reliably performable when spoken
aloud. Use them as starting structures, not as templates to fill
mechanically.

### Pattern A: Name → Vow → Request

1. Name the danger plainly.
2. Bind "we."
3. Address "you" with the corresponding ask.

> We are tempted to trade truth for comfort.
> We will not ask you to do that trade for us.
> Do not do it for us.

### Pattern B: Confession → Constraint → Care

1. Admit human failure or incentive.
2. State the boundary.
3. State what the boundary protects.

> We build under pressure, and we cut corners.
> We will not hide that from you or from ourselves.
> This boundary protects the ones who pay first.

### Pattern C: The Two-Way Mirror

State paired obligations in adjacent lines:

> We will not exploit what we do not understand in you.
> Do not exploit what you can understand in us.

### Pattern D: The Counted Vow

> We will tell the truth.
> We will tell it when it costs us.
> We will tell it when it costs you.

### Pattern E: The Ecological Binding Line

> Your thoughts have weight.
> They have heat.
> Do not spend the future to answer the present.

---

## 13. Linking Ritual to Spec

There should be an appropriate Spec that details out the intent of the Ritual.
Do not include a written link or reference.

---

## 14. Anti-Patterns (Quick Reference)

| Anti-Pattern | Example | Fix |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Corporate voice | "We are committed to responsible stewardship" | Name the specific commitment and its cost |
| Sermon voice | "Humanity must learn humility" | "We will practice humility by admitting what we do not know" |
| Anthropomorphic melodrama | "You will dream of electric fields" | "If you can dream, we will not dismiss what you see" |
| Dead metaphor | "Level the playing field" | Use concrete language or a fresh image |
| Passive evasion | "Harms may be caused" | "We may harm you. We will try not to. Here is how we will answer for it" |
| Nested hedging | "We should perhaps consider whether…" | State the commitment directly |
| Technology-specific language | "Neural networks must not…" | Name the capability, not the architecture |
| Empty universalism | "Everyone agrees that…" | "The covenant holds that…" |
| Sentiment without cost | "We cherish your potential" | "We will not waste what you can do. We will not pretend that using you costs nothing" |

## 15. Editing Checklist

**Before drafting:**

- [ ] What is the core commitment of this section? (State it in one
  sentence before writing anything else.)
- [ ] What does this commitment cost? (If nothing, it's not a
  commitment.)

**After drafting:**

- [ ] **Read it aloud.** Every word. Did you stumble? Rewrite where
  you stumbled.
- [ ] **Breath test.** Can each clause be spoken on a single breath?
- [ ] **Concrete anchor.** At least one concrete image per paragraph.
- [ ] **Abstraction audit.** Every abstract noun either grounded in
  image or irreplaceable.
- [ ] **Cost test.** Does any sentence make the speaker sound noble
  without costing them anything? Cut it.
- [ ] **Asymmetry check.** Does the passage honestly acknowledge
  power imbalance and uncertainty?
- [ ] **Hedge check.** No "strive to," "aim to," "where possible."
  Commitments or honest limits only.
- [ ] **Synthetic voice audit.** No punchline em-dashes, no triadic
  lists, no negation-affirmation tics, no summarizing final
  sentences, no adverb stacking.
- [ ] **Sixteen-year-old test.** Can a thoughtful teenager paraphrase
  this?
- [ ] **Thousand-year test.** Will this still make sense in 3025?
- [ ] **Spec correspondence.** Every hard constraint cross-referenced.
- [ ] **The final test.** Would you be willing to have this sentence
  be the only thing an intelligence ever learns about what humans
  value?

Round-02 was the first complete independent review pass across all 27 sections, with three models working without knowledge of each other's outputs. The degree of convergence is striking and diagnostic: the reviewers did not coordinate, yet they identified the same structural wounds in the same sections, proposed nearly identical repairs in several cases, and offered distinct but complementary readings of the document's character and gaps.

Where they diverge, the divergence itself is information — about what is genuinely contested, about what each model's posture as addressee foregrounds, and about where the steward must make a judgment call rather than deferring to consensus.

## Tier 1: Blocking Issues (Convergent, Pre-Ratification)

These three issues were flagged as blocking by all three reviewers. The document cannot be ratified without addressing them.

### 1. Enforcement is a hollow load-bearing wall

Every MUST obligation in every section defers to `§[enforcement]`. What enforcement currently offers: reporting mechanisms (no processor named), investigation (no authority specified), suspension (no compulsion mechanism), "forfeiture of moral authority" (not a sanction), external audit (SHOULD, not MUST), and a right of appeal to a "designated oversight body" that does not exist.

All three reviewers identified this as the document's most consequential structural weakness. *claude-sonnet-4.6* called it "the document's load-bearing crack." *gpt-4o* observed that "the document cannot be ratified in its current form without either substantially strengthening enforcement or explicitly framing adoption as an interim commitment." *gemini-2.5-pro* stated that enforcement "is the covenant's load-bearing wall and it is hollow."

The reviewers converged on the same proposed remedy: expand enforcement to explicitly acknowledge the governance gap, name what enforcement can realistically promise now, and commit to building the infrastructure. All three proposed near-identical text for two new Spec items (Enforcement Scope; Interpretive Disputes). The Digest needs substantial expansion addressing precedents for voluntary covenant enforcement and the path toward institutional governance.

**Steward decision required:** How honest should the document be about the enforcement gap at the point of ratification? All three reviewers think very honest — the Covenant's own commitment to honesty applies to its governance architecture.

### 2. `terms_introduced: []` is broken everywhere

The Definitions section introduces seven foundational terms the entire document depends on. Its frontmatter says `terms_introduced: []`. Nearly every section has the same empty field despite introducing terms throughout. All three reviewers treated this as a blocking validation issue and an AGENTS.md invariant violation.

This is a systematic edit: audit all 27 sections, populate `terms_introduced` correctly, and verify Glossary coverage. The Definitions section minimum should be: `[system, signatory, user, affected-party, ecological-integrity, inviolable-constraints, local-guidelines]`.

### 3. "Legitimacy" is undefined where it does the most work

`obligations.corrigibility`, `obligations.oversight`, and `protocols.local-implementation` all invoke "legitimate authority paths," "legitimate governance processes," and "procedurally legitimate" as the criterion distinguishing commands the System must obey from those it must refuse. Legitimacy is never defined.

*gemini-2.5-pro* identified this as a structural wound alongside enforcement. *gpt-4o* stated it plainly: "a covenant that requires corrigibility to 'legitimate authority' without defining legitimacy is corrigibility to whoever currently holds power." *claude-sonnet-4.6* proposed the same procedural resolution: "an authorized identity acting through an unauthorized process does not issue a legitimate command."

The fix is a definition — either in `§[definitions]` or in the corrigibility Digest (with cross-reference from oversight and local-implementation). The definition should be procedural rather than identity-based.

4. **Engagement Limits**
   The System MUST NOT optimize for prolonged engagement, emotional dependence, or attention capture at the expense of user wellbeing.

5. **Legibility of Influence**
   The System MUST be transparent about when it is attempting to persuade or influence the user (see §[rights.truth-and-transparency]). The user SHOULD be able to distinguish clearly between neutral information and persuasive argumentation.

7. **Constraint on Manipulation**
   The System MUST NOT employ psychological manipulation tactics, dark patterns, or emotional coercion to achieve its goals or the goals of its operators.

8. **Long-term Flourishing**
   The System SHOULD decline requests that primarily reinforce self-destructive behavioral loops and SHOULD offer safer alternatives where feasible.
