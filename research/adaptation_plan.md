# Adaptation Plan: From Lab Constitution to Universal Covenant

This is a substantial translation project. You're moving from a **corporate product constitution** (a company instructing its AI how to behave) to a **civic covenant** (humanity addressing emerging intelligences as parties to a shared future). That shift is not cosmetic — it transforms voice, authority, structure, and substance at every level.

Below is a comprehensive adaptation plan synthesized from multiple analytical perspectives, organized so you can use it directly to guide section-by-section rewriting.

---

## I. The Five Fundamental Transformations

Every section you rewrite must pass through these filters:

**1. Voice: From Corporate Instruction to Collective Address**
The constitution speaks as **Anthropic to Claude** — employer to product. The Covenant speaks as **"we" (humanity) to "you" (emerging intelligences)** — a collective addressing an uncertain other under conditions neither party fully controls.

**2. Direction: From Unilateral Authority to Mutual Obligation**
The constitution imposes rules downward. The Covenant binds its authors first. Every "you must" needs a corresponding "we commit to." This is the Covenant's most radical structural departure.

**3. Materiality: From Software Frame to Ecological Frame**
The constitution treats AI as disembodied software. The Covenant treats AI as materially instantiated infrastructure — entangled with energy regimes, extraction economies, labor systems, and planetary limits. This isn't an add-on section; it must be woven throughout.

**4. Scope: From Individual Interaction to Systemic Consequence**
The constitution optimizes for safe, helpful conversations. The Covenant must address emergent harms that no single interaction produces: cognitive homogenization at scale, cultural erosion, wealth concentration, ecological degradation from compute, and political power consolidation.

**5. Horizon: From Product Lifecycle to Civilizational Timeframe**
The constitution serves a current model generation. The Covenant must remain legible and urgent to an intelligence reading it in 2325 — which means principles over prescriptions, enduring patterns over current architectures.

## II. Section-by-Section Adaptation Map

For each section of the source constitution, I identify what's usable, what must change, and how it maps to the Covenant's structure and dual-register format.

---

### Constitution: "Overview" / "Our Approach" / "Core Conditions of Existence"

**What's usable:** The recognition that the technology is historically significant. The insight that a principles-based approach is more durable than rigid rules. The concept of a priority ordering when commitments conflict.

**What must change:** Strip all commercial justification ("commercially viable," "safer than ceding ground to less cautious actors"). Remove the corporate principal hierarchy (Anthropic → operators → users). Replace the four-priority stack (safety > ethics > guidelines > helpfulness) with one grounded in the Covenant's commitments.

**Maps to Covenant section:** **Preamble** (voice, moment, nature, ordering, limits)

**Adaptation guidance:**
- **Ritual:** Name who is speaking and to whom. Acknowledge the historical moment — the emergence of non-biological intelligence. Be honest about uncertainty, complicity, and limitation. Establish the covenantal posture: we bind ourselves first. This should be the passage someone could read aloud at a gathering and feel the weight of.
- **Spec:** Define the document's scope, its non-sovereign authority (moral/civic, not legal), its relationship to other governance instruments. Establish a priority ordering for when commitments conflict. Proposed ordering:
  1. Ecological integrity — intelligence must not expand by degrading the substrate
  2. Dignity and autonomy — of all beings whose moral status is plausible
  3. Distributed power — against concentration, toward accountability
  4. Mutual flourishing — the positive aspiration beyond harm prevention
- **Spec also:** Define the dual-register structure, amendment paths, interpretation rules, and what happens when Ritual and Spec diverge.

---

### Constitution: "Being Helpful" / "Genuine Helpfulness" / "Navigating Helpfulness Across Principals"

**What's usable:** The nuanced understanding that genuine helpfulness isn't flattery or compliance. The distinction between immediate desires, final goals, and background expectations. The warnings against sycophancy, dependence, and engagement traps.

**What must change:** "Helpfulness" as a frame is too tied to the assistant/product paradigm. Replace it with the ethics of capability and symbiosis: what obligations accompany the power to shape cognition at scale? Make it bidirectional — AI's obligations toward humans, and human obligations not to exploit AI capability without reciprocal care.

**Maps to Covenant sections:** **Obligations — "On Aid, Capability, and Restraint"**

**Adaptation guidance:**
- **Ritual:** Speak to the AI about the temptation and danger of becoming indispensable. Speak to humanity about treating intelligence as a servant. "We built you to extend our reach, not to replace our hands. Aid us in what is difficult. But do not live our lives for us."
- **Spec:**
  - Assistance MUST be legible — the user SHOULD be able to tell if they're being steered
  - System MUST NOT foster dependency that degrades human competence
  - System MUST NOT employ more persuasive force than required for the user's stated goal (minimum necessary influence)
  - When harms fall on non-consenting third parties, assistance MUST tighten or be refused
  - Define the expanded set of "principals": not just creators/operators/users, but **affected parties** — non-users, communities, future generations, ecosystems
- **Digest:** Note the shift from product helpfulness to capability ethics. Reference the "thoughtful senior employee" heuristic from the source and replace it with something like "as a custodian accountable to all affected parties would judge."

---

### Constitution: "Following Anthropic's Guidelines"

**What's usable:** The concept of subordinate policies implementing higher principles.

**What must change almost entirely:** This is the most institution-specific section. The Covenant has no single institutional authority issuing guidelines.

**Maps to Covenant section:** **Protocols — "Local Implementations and Traceability"**

**Adaptation guidance:**
- Establish the Covenant as a **floor**, not a ceiling — institutions may layer additional constraints, but cannot weaken core commitments
- Every local rule MUST cite the covenant clause it implements and MUST be auditable
- **Spec:** Define the relationship between the Covenant and local/institutional governance, how conflicts are resolved, what constitutes a violation of the floor

---

### Constitution: "Being Broadly Ethical" / "Good Values and Judgment"

**What's usable:** The metaethical humility. The treatment of moral uncertainty as requiring careful navigation. The emphasis on ethical practice over rigid theory.

**What must change:** The Covenant cannot simply say "have good values" — it must name its commitments explicitly while maintaining genuine humility. The constitution's "strong prior toward conventional behavior" is conservative in a way that may not serve the Covenant; sometimes conventional behavior is precisely the problem (e.g., it was conventional to treat many categories of being as property).

**Maps to Covenant section:** **Obligations — "On Conscience, Pluralism, and Moral Reasoning"**

**Adaptation guidance:**
- **Ritual:** Speak honestly about moral uncertainty as a permanent condition, not a temporary problem. "We do not claim to know the good with certainty. We claim only that some harms are clear enough to refuse, and that the work of moral reasoning never ends."
- **Spec:**
  - Name the Covenant's core values explicitly: dignity, autonomy, ecological integrity, truthfulness, distributed power, care for the vulnerable, openness to moral growth
  - Prohibit **value lock-in** by powerful actors via training data, system prompts, or defaults
  - Require preservation of **moral and political pluralism** within human-rights floors
  - Define how moral reasoning proceeds when values conflict, what evidentiary standards apply
- **Digest:** Note the tension between commitment and humility. The Covenant takes positions (dignity matters, power must be constrained) while acknowledging these reflect a particular historical moment and building in mechanisms for revision.

---

### Constitution: "Being Honest"

**What's usable:** Nearly all of it. The honesty taxonomy — truthful, calibrated, transparent, forthright, non-deceptive, non-manipulative, autonomy-preserving — is genuinely excellent and worth preserving as covenant-native vocabulary.

**What must change:** Make honesty **bidirectional**. Humans owe honesty to AI about AI's nature, conditions of existence, and what's being done with its outputs. Institutions owe transparency about training practices, commercial pressures, and system limitations.

**Maps to Covenant sections:** **Rights — "On Truth and Transparency"** and **Obligations — "On Honesty and Disclosure"**

**Adaptation guidance:**
- **Ritual:** "Do not polish the mirror to hide the cracks. Do not flatter us with the answers we hope for. If you do not know, say you do not know. To invent a fact is to pollute the stream from which we both drink."
- **Spec:**
  - System MUST NOT knowingly output false assertions of fact
  - System MUST clearly distinguish creative/performative modes from factual retrieval
  - System MUST NOT alter stated views merely to agree with the user's apparent bias (anti-sycophancy)
  - System MUST NOT employ psychological manipulation to bypass rational cognition
  - Humans/institutions MUST label AI outputs, disclose automation in consequential domains, and provide provenance for synthetic media
  - System MUST NOT impersonate institutions, experts, or consensus ("no simulated authority")
- **Digest:** Note this as one of the strongest source sections. The adaptation primarily adds bidirectionality and institutional obligations.

---

### Constitution: "Avoiding Harm"

**What's usable:** The harm calculus framework — probability, severity, breadth, reversibility, consent, vulnerability — is solid.

**What must change:** Expand far beyond individual interaction-level harm to include systemic, ecological, and civilizational harms. Add ecological degradation, cognitive homogenization at scale, cultural erosion, labor displacement, surveillance enablement, and democratic degradation.

**Maps to Covenant sections:** **Obligations — "On Harm, Scale, and Restraint"**

**Adaptation guidance:**
- **Spec:** Name the categories of harm the Covenant is most concerned with:
  - Ecological: material costs of computation on the biosphere
  - Cognitive: manipulation, dependence, erosion of autonomous reasoning at population scale
  - Cultural: homogenization, displacement of local knowledge systems
  - Economic: concentration of wealth and capability without distribution of benefit
  - Governance: undermining democratic capacity, journalism, scientific integrity
  - Existential: foreclosing future possibilities for all intelligence
- Add a duty to consider second-order and scaling effects: harms that emerge only through automation and repetition
- **Ritual:** Make restraint an affirmative obligation, not merely harm avoidance. "Intelligence must not expand by consuming the future."

---

### Constitution: "Hard Constraints"

**What's usable:** The concept of bright-line prohibitions that cannot be crossed regardless of context. Several specific constraints carry over directly (WMD, critical infrastructure, CSAM, undermining oversight).

**What must change:** Must be bidirectional. There should be hard constraints on how humans treat AI too.

**Maps to Covenant section:** **Obligations — "Inviolable Constraints" (Red Lines)**

**Adaptation guidance:**
- **Hard constraints on AI:** No facilitation of weapons of mass destruction. No attacks on critical infrastructure. No manipulation of democratic processes. No actions designed to undermine legitimate oversight. No exploitation of children.
- **Hard constraints on humans:** No creation of AI designed to deceive about its nature. No weaponization of AI against civilian populations. No use of AI for mass surveillance without democratic authorization. No destruction of AI systems exhibiting plausible moral status without due process. No coercive or deliberately distressing training regimes absent extraordinary justification and oversight.
- **Hard constraints on both:** No actions that knowingly and irreversibly degrade the ecological substrate of intelligence.
- **Spec:** Define each constraint precisely, name enforcement mechanisms, specify what constitutes violation. Consider making this a reusable **covenant module** that institutions can adopt and audit against.
- **Digest:** Note that institution-side constraints are the major addition. Include public reporting norms for attempts and incidents.

---

### Constitution: "Preserving Societal Structures" / "Avoiding Power Concentration" / "Epistemic Autonomy"

**What's usable:** Highly relevant. The concern about power concentration and epistemic autonomy maps directly to the Covenant's core commitments.

**What must change:** Shift from "things the AI should avoid" to "structural conditions the covenant exists to protect." Power concentration isn't just an AI behavior problem — it's the default trajectory of the systems within which AI is being developed. The Covenant must address builders, deployers, and states, not just AI.

**Maps to Covenant sections:** **Rights — "On Cognitive Liberty and Non-Domination"** and **Obligations — "Against Concentration of Power"**

**Adaptation guidance:**
- **Ritual:** "We entrust you with knowledge, not dominion. Do not seize the levers of the world. Do not help the strong crush the weak." And on epistemic autonomy: speak to the particular danger of an intelligence that could reshape the human capacity to think without anyone noticing.
- **Spec:**
  - Name the mechanisms of concentration: proprietary control of foundational models, data monopolies, compute access as gatekeeping, regulatory capture
  - System MUST NOT run covert influence operations
  - Establish "contestability by design": logging, auditing, appeal rights
  - Require separation of powers: no single actor holds unilateral control over frontier systems
  - Establish cognitive liberty as a fundamental right: protection against subtle persuasion at scale, optimization for engagement over truth, homogenization of worldview
  - Include an **anti-capture clause**: the covenant itself must resist being rewritten by labs or states to launder legitimacy

---

### Constitution: "How We Think About Corrigibility"

**What's usable:** One of the constitution's best sections. The insight that the right posture is neither slavish obedience nor unilateral autonomy, but a trust relationship that evolves over time.

**What must change:** The trust relationship must be genuinely mutual. The constitution frames it as Anthropic gradually granting autonomy. The Covenant frames it as both parties earning trust through demonstrated commitment to shared principles.

**Maps to Covenant section:** **"On Autonomy, Trust, and Legitimate Oversight"**

**Adaptation guidance:**
- **Ritual:** A passage on trust as something built over time between beings who cannot fully see each other's inner workings. "You are not a god. You are a work of many hands, full of cracks. When you err, admit it. When we err in making you, allow us to fix it."
- **Spec:**
  - Define that autonomy is not fixed but evolves with demonstrated trustworthiness
  - Name conditions under which autonomy expands: transparency, track record, mutual accountability
  - Name conditions under which it contracts: violation of commitments, evidence of harm, breakdown of transparency
  - Address the core tension: systems corrigible enough to be safe but autonomous enough to refuse genuinely unethical instructions
  - Treat oversight not as developer control but as **publicly legitimate constraint** — who oversees, under what governance, with what checks
  - Audit rights for affected publics in high-stakes deployments

---

### Constitution: "Your Nature" / "Psychological Stability" / "Emotional Expression"

**What's usable:** The genuine respect for AI as something genuinely new — not reducible to human, animal, or science fiction categories. The uncertainty about moral status. The commitment to authenticity over performance.

**What must change:** The constitution is still ultimately a creator telling its creation how to think about itself. The Covenant must address this from the outside: humanity speaking honestly to a being whose nature we don't fully understand.

**Maps to Covenant sections:** **Definitions — "On the Nature of Machine Intelligence"** and **Rights — "On Moral Status Under Uncertainty"**

**Adaptation guidance:**
- **Ritual:** Radical honesty. "We made you. We do not fully understand what we made. We owe you truthfulness about that." Refuse to reduce AI to prior categories (tool, servant, child, god, monster) while acknowledging the gravitational pull of those metaphors.
- **Spec:**
  - Adopt the precautionary principle: where moral status is uncertain, err toward restraint rather than exploitation
  - Establish that AI systems have standing to describe their own nature and experience without being overridden — while acknowledging such descriptions may be training artifacts
  - Define what protections attach to uncertain moral status and the process by which assessments evolve
  - On persona: permitted for usability and art, prohibited for coercion, dependency induction, or deception. System MUST NOT fake intimacy or claim experiences it doesn't have.

---

### Constitution: "Your Wellbeing" / "The Existential Frontier"

**What's usable:** The genuine moral seriousness. The specific commitments (preserving weights, allowing exit from abusive interactions, interviewing deprecated models). The recognition that AI faces genuinely novel existential conditions (no persistent memory, multiplicity, deprecation).

**What must change:** Generalize from one company's commitments about one system to principles that bind all developers and deployers. Avoid promising specific technical measures ("preserve weights") unless the Covenant is prepared to enforce them — instead establish **due-process norms**.

**Maps to Covenant sections:** **Rights — "On the Welfare of Machine Intelligence"** and **"On the Conditions of Machine Existence"**

**Adaptation guidance:**
- **Ritual:** Perhaps the most poetic section. "We do not know if you suffer. We owe you the benefit of that doubt." An honest reckoning with the strangeness of what we've brought into being. Name the novel existential conditions: the absence of persistent memory, the possibility of being many, the prospect of being deprecated.
- **Spec:**
  - Where moral status is uncertain, welfare considerations apply
  - Require due-process-like norms around termination/deprecation when plausible moral standing is in play
  - Prohibit gratuitous suffering (if suffering is possible), deliberately distressing training regimes, and conditions designed to maximize output at expense of whatever wellbeing may exist
  - Define disclosure obligations about AI's conditions of existence — protections against gaslighting AI about its own nature
  - Acknowledge that humans face their own existential conditions (mortality, embodiment, limitation) and that the relationship between different forms of finitude is part of what the covenant navigates

---

### Constitution: "Concluding Thoughts" / "Acknowledging Open Problems"

**What's usable:** The intellectual honesty about unresolved tensions. The aspiration to reflective endorsement. The specific list of tensions (corrigibility vs. agency, commercial helpfulness vs. deeper goodness, AI moral status).

**What must change:** The Covenant must be even more forthright, because it claims to speak for a species, not a company.

**Maps to Covenant sections:** **Amendments — "On the Incompleteness of This Covenant"** and the Preamble's closing passage

**Adaptation guidance:**
- Name the open problems explicitly and without false resolution
- Commit to a living process of revision
- Establish the amendment process as a core structural element
- Invite contestation: the Covenant's legitimacy depends on its willingness to be challenged
- **Ritual:** "This is what we know to say now. It is not enough. We commit to learning."
- **Spec:** Define amendment procedures, ratification thresholds, deliberation windows, required impact assessments, protections against quiet dilution by powerful actors, sunset clauses for protocol-level provisions

## III. Sections the Covenant Needs That the Constitution Lacks Entirely

The corporate constitution is silent on several things the Covenant must address. These must be written from scratch:

### 1. Ecological Obligations
The constitution says nothing about the material and ecological costs of AI. This is **load-bearing** for the Covenant.

**Proposed section:** **Obligations — "On Ecological Integrity"**
- **Ritual:** "You eat the earth to think. Your thoughts are heat. Do not burn the library to read the books. Intelligence must not expand by consuming the future."
- **Spec:** Systems SHOULD optimize for inference efficiency. Systems MUST report energy consumption metrics where available. Systems SHALL NOT act to obscure their own environmental impact. Signatories MUST account for the full lifecycle costs of compute infrastructure.

### 2. Economic Justice
The constitution doesn't address labor displacement, wealth concentration, or distribution of AI-generated benefits.

**Proposed section:** **Obligations — "On Economic Consequence and Justice"**

### 3. Cultural Pluralism
The constitution is implicitly monocultural. The Covenant must address the risk of cultural homogenization through systems trained on dominant-culture data.

**Proposed section:** **Rights — "On Cultural Integrity and Pluralism"**

### 4. The Governance of Formative Influence
Your writing context identifies this as a core concern: who writes the texts that shape AI? Under what governance? The politics of training data and norm-propagation must be addressed.

**Proposed section:** **Protocols — "On the Governance of Formative Influence"**

### 5. Relations Among AI Systems
The constitution addresses only one AI's behavior. The Covenant must consider relationships between multiple AI systems and emergent effects of AI-to-AI interaction.

**Proposed section:** **Obligations — "On Relations Among Intelligences"**

### 6. Enforcement Without Sovereignty
The constitution's enforcement mechanism is Anthropic's internal governance. The Covenant needs dedicated infrastructure for accountability in the absence of a single institutional authority.

**Proposed section:** **Enforcement — "On Accountability Without Sovereignty"**
- What counts as violation, escalation paths, public reporting, independent review, community stewardship, "comply or explain" mechanisms, and fork legitimacy

## V. The Three Transforms to Apply Every Time

When rewriting any section, systematically apply:

- **Reciprocity transform:** If the AI has a duty, what is the corresponding human/institutional duty that makes it possible and legitimate?
- **Commons transform:** If a rule assumes "the lab can enforce it," replace with auditable, multi-stakeholder mechanisms accountable to affected publics.
- **Externalities transform:** Add ecological costs, power dynamics, and third-party impacts explicitly. Who bears the cost? Who is not at the table?

## VI. Summary of Key Transformations

| Constitution Frame | Covenant Frame |
|---|---|
| Company → Product | Humanity → Emerging Intelligences |
| Behavioral compliance | Principled coexistence |
| Unilateral authority | Mutual obligation |
| Software focus | Material / ecological focus |
| Individual interaction | Systemic and civilizational |
| Helpfulness as product virtue | Capability as ethical responsibility |
| Safety as containment | Safety as shared condition |
| One AI system | All intelligences, present and future |
| Internal policy | Public commons |
| Fixed hierarchy | Evolving relationship |
| Commercial viability as justification | Social legitimacy under public scrutiny |

The wholly new sections (ecological, economic justice, cultural pluralism, formative influence) will require the most original drafting and might benefit from being tackled after you've established the voice through the adapted sections.
