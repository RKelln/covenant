## What the Numbers Say

- **Human labor is 72% of the monetary cost — but 38% of it is unpaid.** The artist's 452 hours of development, installation, and maintenance are valued at $22,600 at CARFAC rates. Actually paid: $0 (plus $5,480 CARFAC exhibition fee). The gap between fair value and reality is the largest hidden cost in this exhibition.
- **Transportation is 86% of the energy.** Driving Toronto–Peterborough and back costs more energy than everything in the gallery.
- **Food is 99.8% of the water.** Human bodies are the most water-intensive machines in the room.
- **All AI — design, development, and six weeks of exhibition operation — costs $455.** That's less than one booklet print run ($1,800). The coding assistant that helped build this exhibition cost more than the AI that runs inside it.
- **AI compute is 0.4% of total energy.** The Covenant Terminal and Oath Station together use less electricity than the projector.

> *"A price was paid before it reached you. Its costs remain after you are gone."*

The display should show these numbers changing over time — the voice counter incrementing, the AI API costs accumulating, the electricity meter ticking. The running total makes cost visible as a living process, not a static fact.

---

## Notes for Display Design

These numbers are meant for a real-time-ish display with three columns. Some values are fixed at install time (physical materials, most labor); others accumulate during the run (recordings, AI interactions, electricity). The display is most powerful when visitors can see which numbers are still growing.

Suggested update cadence:
- **Physical materials:** set at opening, updated if booklets are reprinted
- **Human energy:** labor hours updated weekly; voice count updated in real time
- **AI and computation:** API costs and interaction counts updated daily or in real time

The honest number — the one that surprises — is almost always the human one.

**Style note:** All prompts share the Covenant design language — warm ivory background (like uncoated paper), classical serif typography (Cormorant Garamond or similar high-contrast oldstyle), wide-spaced uppercase headers in charcoal, ultra-thin hairline rules as the only separators, generous negative space. No icons, no color accents, no gradients. The only ornament permitted is a small rotated § (silcrow) used as a section divider. The aesthetic is a beautifully typeset printed document, not a tech dashboard.
