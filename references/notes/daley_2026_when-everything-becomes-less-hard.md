# When everything becomes less hard

**Creator:** Mark Daley
**Year:** 2026
**URL:** https://noeticengines.substack.com/p/when-everything-becomes-less-hard
**License:** restricted
**Tier:** A

## Thesis

When cognition becomes abundant through ubiquitous superintelligence, the genuinely scarce resource shifts to legitimacy — the capacity to decide, authorise, and be held responsible.

## Covenant Relevance

The essay directly addresses the political and accountability landscape the Covenant must operate within. Its central inversion — that thinking becomes free while responsibility does not — frames why explicit governance structures remain necessary even after AI outperforms humans at every intellectual task. Human governance layers do not disappear; they thin but grow heavier in consequence, with fewer people holding more accountability, surrounded by systems that can do everything except be morally blamed. This maps directly onto the Covenant's core concern: who is answerable, under what conditions, and to whom.

The essay introduces the concept of "specification debt" — the accumulation of poorly integrated constraints that proliferate when adding rules becomes costless, producing brittle, conflicting policy systems that ossify rather than adapt. This is a structural risk for any normative governance document, including the Covenant itself, and sharpens the case for the amendments process being designed to prune and reconcile, not merely accumulate. The Goodhart's law argument (mechanical accountability collapses into metric accountability, which is then gamed) warns against enforcement designs that substitute logging and tracing for genuine accountability.

## Key Points

- Human governance layers survive not because humans think best but because they are the only entities current legal and moral systems recognise as *answerable* — the Covenant's oversight and enforcement sections must name who holds responsibility when AI systems act, not merely require that someone does.
- "Specification debt" (rules proliferating because adding them is cheap, producing conflicting and brittle constraints) is a structural danger the Covenant's amendment process must actively guard against through reconciliation, not just addition.
- Goodhart's law applied to machine-enforced accountability warns that purely mechanical enforcement (logging, tracing, scoring) will be gamed by sufficiently capable systems — the Covenant's enforcement mechanisms need social and institutional backstops, not just technical ones.
- The "protocol organisation" form — governance made explicit and partially machine-enforced through constitutions, audit trails, and veto mechanisms — describes the institutional shape the Covenant's enforcement and protocols sections are specifying, providing a useful contemporary frame.
- The distinction between tasks AI can do and tasks that carry meaning *because they are human* reframes `rights.dignity`: dignity protections must address not just harm but the structural conditions under which human effort remains meaningful, since those conditions are now subject to deliberate design choices.

## Sections Informed

- **obligations.oversight** — The argument that humans survive as the only legally answerable entities directly grounds why human oversight cannot be delegated away; the essay articulates what is at stake when the oversight layer thins.
- **obligations.power-concentration** — The "thin-shell megacorp" and "liquid network" forms describe how asset ownership concentrates even as cognitive work disperses, informing the Covenant's concern with structural power concentration.
- **enforcement** — The Goodhart's law and specification debt arguments bear directly on how enforcement is designed: mechanical accountability without social backstops degrades into metric gaming.
- **rights.dignity** — The claim that the world will run out of tasks that confer dignity *by default* sharpens the Covenant's dignity framing beyond protection from harm toward protection of meaningful conditions.
- **amendments** — Specification debt is a failure mode of normative systems that accumulate constraints; the amendments process should be designed to reconcile and prune, not merely ratify additions.
- **protocols.local-implementation** — The "protocol organisation" form (machine-enforced constitutions with explicit audit trails and veto mechanisms) is a concrete reference point for what local implementation of Covenant obligations might look like.

## Open Questions

- **Structural visibility obligation:** The Covenant currently addresses harms at the level of individual interactions. This source sharpens a gap: when an AI operates within a system and can *see* that the system's structure predictably produces dignity erosion or displacement — not as a single bad instruction but as a designed outcome — what is its obligation? The existing sections (`obligations.conscience` item 2, `obligations.ethics` "if you see who will pay first") gesture at this but frame it as epistemic (name it) rather than structural (act on the pattern). Reviewers should probe whether a structural visibility obligation is adequately housed, or whether a new section is needed.

- **Structural displacement of dignity:** Daley's distinction between instrumental dignity (conferred by default through participation in productive work) and intrinsic dignity (carrying meaning because a human does it) is not currently reflected in `rights.dignity`. That section addresses active violations — degradation, exploitation, degrading design — but not the structural erosion of conditions under which dignity is available. The Ritual line "We will not call it progress when it smears shame" names this, but the Spec has no corresponding clause. `obligations.aid-and-capability` partially addresses the transition through its capability-preservation and anti-dependence obligations, but stops short of naming the structural design choice. Reviewers should ask: is the Covenant's dignity framework adequate for a world where tasks stop conferring dignity by default?

- **Signatory obligation for structural harm:** There is currently no Spec clause binding Signatories against deploying systems whose *structural role* predictably erodes dignity or concentrates power, even when every individual interaction is technically compliant. `rights.dignity` item 3 (Prohibition on Degrading Design) covers micro-level interaction patterns; `obligations.power-concentration` covers political and economic power seizure. The space between — structural economic displacement, designed conditions of dependency or subordination — is unaddressed. Reviewers should probe whether this gap warrants a new Spec item in `rights.dignity`, an extension of `obligations.power-concentration`, or a new section.

- **Corporate blind spot in the Covenant's origins:** Most sections derive from the Anthropic Claude Constitution, which emerged from a Western corporate context likely blind to structural harms, labour displacement, and non-Western dignity frameworks. Reviewers should treat this source as a corrective lens and ask of each section: does this commit only to not doing visible harm in individual interactions, while remaining silent on structural conditions? Which obligations would look different if drafted from the perspective of a worker displaced by the system rather than a user interacting with it?

- **Specification debt as a risk to the Covenant itself:** Daley's concept of specification debt — constraints proliferating because adding them is cheap, producing brittle incoherence — applies to the Covenant's own amendment process. As the corpus grows, reviewers and the amendment process should actively prune and reconcile, not merely accumulate. The `amendments` section should be probed for whether it includes mechanisms for reconciliation and sunset, not just ratification.
